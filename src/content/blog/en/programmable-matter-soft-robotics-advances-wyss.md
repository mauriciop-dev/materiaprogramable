The Wyss Institute for Biologically Inspired Engineering at Harvard continues to lead the frontier of programmable matter by developing additive manufacturing techniques that allow complex functions to be integrated directly into the structure of materials. A recent and significant advancement is the multimaterial rotational 3D printing technique, which enables the creation of soft robots and structures capable of deforming in a predictable and programmed manner when activated, eliminating the need for traditional molds or complicated mechanical assemblies.

This technology uses a dual nozzle that simultaneously extrudes a flexible polyurethane skeleton and a soluble poloxamer gel core. The innovation lies in the nozzle's ability to rotate and reorient filaments during the printing process, which allows "programming" the direction in which the material will bend or expand once the internal gel is removed and replaced with pressurized air. This approach transforms static objects into active matter that can respond to pneumatic stimuli to perform gripping or locomotion tasks.

Beyond the physical architecture, the Wyss Institute has integrated artificial intelligence-based control systems that allow these soft matter systems to learn and adjust their movements in real-time. This combination of "material intelligence" (structural design) and "computational intelligence" is a critical step toward functional programmable matter capable of adapting to dynamic environments, such as in minimally invasive robotic surgery applications or human assistance devices that require safe and flexible interaction.

The potential of these research efforts for 2025-2026 is not limited to the macroscale. The promising advances in the miniaturization of these systems open the door to internal medical applications, where soft robots the size of capsules could navigate the gastrointestinal tract or perform precise interventions without the need for invasive surgeries. The convergence of 4D printing, AI, and soft robotics at the Wyss Institute is shaping the future of programmable matter toward an era of truly intelligent and self-sufficient materials.
