Imagine if the physical world had an "update" button. Programmable matter is not a static material with a single function; it is a category of systems that can change their physical properties -such as shape, density, color, or conductivity- in a controlled manner through algorithms. Essentially, we are talking about transforming hardware into something as flexible and updatable as the software running on our phones. It is no longer just about fabricating objects, but about "compiling" them from intelligent fundamental units.

The most robust technical concept in this area is Claytronics, which proposes the creation of Catoms (claytronic atoms). These are modular micro-robots that act as "physical pixels". Just as pixels on a retina display group together to form a coherent image, thousands of catoms can join via electrostatic or magnetic forces to form a solid three-dimensional object. These objects can have touch-sensitive skin, light sensors, and the ability to transmit information, turning any tool into an intelligent and dynamic device.

Unlike traditional robotics, where a single robot performs a task, programmable matter is based on swarm intelligence and distributed systems. Each minimal unit has its own computing capacity, but the "behavior" of the final object emerges from massive cooperation. If a component is damaged, the system can reconfigure itself to fill the void, giving physical objects a self-repair and resilience capability previously seen only in biological organisms.

This transition from subtractive (removing material) and additive (3D printing) manufacturing to reconfigurable manufacturing will change our relationship with waste. If a chair is no longer needed, its components do not go to the trash; they simply receive a new code instruction to disassemble and reorganize into a nightstand or a lamp. We are facing the end of physical obsolescence of objects, shifting the value from material to the design and code that governs it.
